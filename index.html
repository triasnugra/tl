🗝Your Core Message as a TL
  • Set a high bar: I don't want a team that just "passes." I want a team that actually grows and does their best work.
  • Remove what blocks them: If agent are slow or making mistakes, I ask why first — is it the SOP? The tools? The training?
  • Trust their judgment: SOPs are a starting point, not the final word. I want my team to think, not just follow steps blindly.
  • Multiply the team: My job as TL is not to be the best person anymore — it's to help people do their best.

🏛How You Lead Step by Step
1
Look at the Data First
Before doing anything, I check the numbers — AHT, accuracy, productivity. I look for patterns, not just individual bad days.
2
Find the Root Cause
Is it a knowledge gap? A motivation problem? A personal issue? I need to know the real reason before I give any solution.
3
Apply the Right Fix, Then Check Again
I give the right support — coaching, refreshment, or a personal talk — then I follow up to see if it's working. If not, I adjust.
🚀What Makes You Different as a TL
  • You've done the job: i was an agent. i know what it's like to struggle with a hard case. Use that to understand my agents better.
  • Empathy with standards: i care about your team's feelings, but i also hold them to a good standard. It's not one or the other — it's both.
  • Teach, don't just tell: When an agent makes mistakes, i don't just say "follow the SOP." Help them understand why the SOP exists and how to use their own judgment.
  • Your goal is their growth: i want at least one agent from your team to be ready for a Team Support role in the future. That's how i measure real success.
⚙️How You Improve Team Performance
1
Check What's Slowing Them Down
I look for where agents are getting stuck. Is the SOP unclear? Is the tool slow? Do they not have enough knowledge?
2
Fix the Hard Cases First
The difficult cases are the ones that take the most time. If I solve those, the team's AHT will drop the most. Focus on the hardest problems, not the easy ones.
3
Share the Solution with Everyone
When I fix one person's problem, I share it with the whole team. That way, the same issue doesn't happen again for someone else.

⚠️Handling a Mod Who Is Struggling
  • Bad performance but good attitude? I'll always choose this person. Skills can be taught. Attitude is much harder to change.
  • Find the exact problem: I don't just say "they're bad at the job." I look at specific cases — where exactly do they make mistakes, and why?
  • Give them a real chance: I give them support and a clear goal to hit. If they're willing to try, I'm willing to help.
  • Be honest if it's not working: If an agent keeps having a bad attitude and refuses to change, I follow the proper steps — watchlist, warnings, and HR if needed.
🚪When an agent Wants to Resign
1
Find Out the Real Reason
A resignation is usually the last step, not the first sign. I ask early — are they feeling burned out, bored, or is something bothering them at work?
2
Offer a Real Solution, Not Just Words
If it's about performance, I offer coaching and a clearer path. If it's about the work environment, I try to fix what I can. I don't just say "it'll get better."
3
Let Them Leave With Respect
If they still want to go, I make sure the exit is smooth and professional. How someone leaves matters for the team's morale too.
⚡Tough Situations — What You'd Do
  • Client raises the target: I won't just push the team to go faster. First, I sit down with other TLs, ACCM, and WFM to plan together. Then I raise the target step by step — not all at once — so quality doesn't drop.
  • Managing your former friends: On the floor, I'm professional. Off the floor, we're still friends. I tell them clearly from the start: "I care about you, and that's exactly why I have to be fair to everyone."
  • Two mods are fighting: I don't take sides. I give them space to calm down, listen to both of them separately, then bring them together to talk it out. I remind them: "We don't have to be best friends, but we have to respect each other at work."
🕒When Things Get Chaotic
  • Handling a shift alone or working late: I prepare by knowing exactly what needs to be done. For complex cases, I ask the strongest agent to help. I track how fast everyone works so I can plan better next time. 1–2 extended sessions is okay — I solve it as fast as possible and learn from it.
  • Suspicious sick leave: I care about my team's health, so I always ask for proof. But I also check patterns — if someone is always "sick" right before/after their day off, that's a red flag. I'm kind to those who are genuinely unwell, but fair to the rest of the team too.

LeadershipWhat is your leadership style? ▼
"I lead in a democratic way — but that doesn't mean I just let the team decide everything. It means I actually listen to them. My agents are the ones doing the work every day, so their feedback is really valuable. I take that input, think it through, and then make a clear decision. I balance three things: communication — I keep conversations open and two-way; fairness — I treat everyone equally and follow the guidelines; and flexibility — I adapt to each person's needs without being too strict or too soft."
OperationsHow do you handle an agent who keeps missing KPI? ▼
"First, I don't just jump to a solution — I find out why they're struggling. Is it a knowledge problem? Do they not understand the SOP? Or is something going on in their personal life? Once I know the root cause, I give the right kind of help. If it's knowledge, I work with TQA for a refreshment session and do live monitoring with them. If it's personal, I give them space and support. I also set small weekly goals so they can see their own progress. And I always appreciate the effort, not just the result. If nothing works after all of this, I follow the proper process — watchlist, warnings, and escalation to ACCM."
TeamBad performance but good attitude, or good performance but bad attitude — which do you pick? ▼
"I'd always choose the person with bad performance but a good attitude. Here's why: performance is something we can fix. With the right training, coaching, and time, most people can improve. But attitude is much harder to change. If someone has a bad attitude, it doesn't just affect their own work — it brings the whole team down. I'd rather coach someone who's willing to learn than manage someone who doesn't want to be there. I can teach skills. It's very hard to teach someone to care."
OperationsWhat are your goals as a Team Leader? ▼
"My first goal is to make sure my team consistently hits KPI — accuracy, AHT, and productivity. But beyond the numbers, I want to develop each person on my team. I want everyone to understand their work deeply, not just follow steps. And long-term? I want my team to produce the next Career Level Up. If someone I coached gets promoted within a preferenced time to career jump (lets say 6 months), that's my biggest achievement as a TL. I don't want to be the only one who grows — I want to grow together with my team."

TeamHow do you maintain a professional boundary with mods who used to be your friends? ▼
"This is something I take seriously. On the floor, I have to be professional — that means equal treatment for everyone, no special favors, and clear expectations. Off the floor, we can still have a good relationship. I would tell my close colleagues early on: 'Nothing about our friendship changes, but my responsibilities do. I have to be fair to everyone, and I hope you understand that.' I think most people respect that kind of honesty. Fairness is how you earn respect as a new leader — not by being the most popular."
🎤How to Speak With Confidence
  • Don't read from notes: Practice your answers until they feel natural. Notes make you look unsure. Speaking from memory shows you believe what you're saying.
  • Pause before you answer: Take 2–3 seconds to think. It makes you look calm and thoughtful, not nervous.
  • Use real examples: Instead of saying "I'm good at handling outlier mods," say "When I had a mod who kept missing AHT, here's what I did…" Specifics are much more convincing.
  • Own your weakness: "I'm new to leadership, but that's exactly why I'm here — I want to learn it properly and grow." Confidence is about being honest, not being perfect.
  • Speak slower than you think: When we're nervous, we talk too fast. Slow down on purpose. It helps you sound clearer and more in control.
